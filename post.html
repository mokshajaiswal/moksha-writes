Why I stopped designing for perfection and started designing for meaning

After years of chasing pixel-perfect outcomes, I discovered that the best work emerges from a very different kind of intention — one rooted in curiosity, not control.

8 min read

The best work I've ever done didn't come from a brief. It didn't come from a client's vision board or a competitor analysis or a perfectly scoped design sprint.

It came from a moment of genuine confusion — staring at a screen at 11pm, wondering why everything I was making felt technically correct but emotionally inert. I had nailed the grid. I had the right typeface. The spacing was immaculate. And somehow, none of it meant anything.

That was the night I started asking different questions.

The obsession with perfect

When I started designing, I was obsessed with perfection. I think most designers are. You spend years developing taste — learning to see the difference between good and great, between careless and considered — and then you spend the rest of your career trying to live up to the standard you've built in your head.

The problem with this isn't the high bar. High bars are useful. The problem is what they do to your decision-making process. Perfectionism narrows your vision. It makes you optimise for the artifact instead of the impact. It keeps you inside the frame of the work instead of asking whether the frame is the right shape to begin with.

The gap between technically correct and genuinely resonant is invisible in screenshots.

I have a folder of work I'm "proud of" — work that won compliments in reviews, work that shipped beautifully, work I could put in a portfolio without apology. And when I look at it now, a lot of it feels like a very skilled answer to the wrong question.

What changed everything

The shift started with a project for a fintech startup. We were designing an onboarding flow — the kind of work I'd done a dozen times. Patterns I knew. Principles I understood. I was halfway through the first round of screens when the founder, almost as an aside, mentioned why she'd started the company.

"The goal isn't for people to understand the product. The goal is for them to feel like they can trust money again."

I went back to the screens. Nothing in what I'd designed was addressing that. It was clean, it was functional, it was consistent — and it was solving entirely the wrong problem.

That experience rewired something for me. I started treating emotional intent as a design constraint — not a nice-to-have, not a brand consideration someone else handled, but a primary input that shapes every decision from layout to language to the weight of a button label.

Emotion as a design primitive — what does it feel like to succeed at this task?

The practice

This isn't an argument against craft. I still care deeply about execution — about the pixel and the spacing and the motion curve. But I now treat craft as a vehicle, not a destination.

The question I ask myself at the start of every project has changed. Instead of "what does the ideal design look like?" I ask: "what should this feel like to succeed at?" It sounds subtle, but it completely reorients how I approach the work.

When you design for success feeling, you make different choices. You notice different things in research. You ask different questions in critique. You argue for different things in stakeholder reviews — and you know why you're arguing for them, which makes all the difference.

Some practical things that shifted:

  • I write an emotional brief before any visual work. Not a mood board. A brief. What does the user feel coming in? What do we want them to feel leaving?
  • I audit my own work for emotional resonance, not just usability. Does this feel trustworthy? Does this feel like it respects my time?
  • I push back harder on scope creep that adds features but dilutes feeling. More options aren't always more empowering — sometimes they're just more noise.

Moving forward

I don't think perfectionism disappears. It just needs to be redirected. The standard worth obsessing over isn't "is this visually correct?" — it's "does this work in the way that actually matters to the person using it?"

Some of my best-received work is, by traditional metrics, imperfect. It has rough edges. It took shortcuts that would make a design-system purist wince. But it trusted its users, and they felt it.

That's the kind of perfection I'm interested in now.
